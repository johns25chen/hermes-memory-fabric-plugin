## Knowledge Surface Candidates

### photoshop_retouching_core_methodology

High-value Photoshop retouching knowledge should be organized around
non-destructive control and visual diagnosis:

1. Work non-destructively: use duplicate layers, smart objects, adjustment
   layers, masks, and separate retouch layers where the tool supports sampling
   from all layers. Preserve the original image.
2. Clean before stylizing: remove temporary distractions, dust, blemishes,
   flyaway hair, sensor spots, and edge artifacts before color grading or
   contrast work.
3. Separate texture from tone when needed: frequency separation is valuable
   when texture and color/tone need independent correction. The high-frequency
   layer carries fine detail such as pores, hair, grain, and texture; the
   low-frequency layer carries broader color, tone, and shadow transitions.
4. Prefer subtle dodge and burn for shape and skin realism: curve-based dodge
   and burn layers allow local brightening/darkening while preserving texture.
   This is often the most natural path for high-end skin work.
5. Avoid plastic skin: over-blurring, excessive frequency separation, global
   smoothing, and heavy AI beautification can destroy skin texture and make
   faces look artificial.
6. Retouch at multiple zoom levels: inspect defects up close, but repeatedly
   check the full image for expression, anatomy, silhouette, lighting direction,
   and overall believability.
7. Use diagnostic views: black-and-white check layers, contrast curves, or
   solar-curve-style checks can reveal uneven tone and retouching artifacts.
8. Finish with restrained global work: color grade, sharpen, vignette, crop,
   and export only after local cleanup and tonal correction are stable.

### photoshop_ai_assisted_retouching_methodology

Adobe Photoshop generative tools should be treated as targeted retouching
assistants, not as replacements for image judgment:

1. Use Generative Fill or Remove-style workflows for specific distractions,
   missing background, object removal, and small reconstruction tasks.
2. Use Generative Expand for canvas extension and composition repair, then
   inspect perspective, repeating texture, shadows, and edge continuity.
3. Keep edits bounded by selection and mask discipline. Prompt only the desired
   change, and preserve the subject, identity, perspective, lens feel, and
   lighting unless intentionally changing them.
4. Reconcile AI fills manually when needed: clone, heal, mask, dodge/burn, and
   color-correct the generated area so it matches the original photograph.
5. Treat text, logos, faces, hands, jewelry, product labels, and brand marks as
   high-risk details requiring manual verification.

### gpt_image_2_generation_methodology

GPT Image 2 / ChatGPT Images 2.0 prompting should be handled as a production
brief:

1. Define the asset purpose first: product image, portrait, storyboard panel,
   UI mockup, poster, ad concept, thumbnail, reference sheet, or print asset.
2. Specify subject, environment, action, composition, framing, lighting,
   material, style, mood, output format, and restrictions.
3. For text-bearing images, provide exact wording, hierarchy, placement,
   language, and typography direction; verify the final text manually.
4. Use aspect ratio and format constraints up front. Where supported, configure
   size, quality, output format, compression, and background/transparency
   rather than relying on prompt wording alone.
5. Keep the negative constraints focused: no watermark, no extra text, no
   distorted logo, no duplicate people, no deformed hands, no warped geometry,
   no unrelated objects, or other task-specific failures.
6. Generate variants only for meaningful uncertainty such as composition,
   lighting, or style direction. Do not create large batches without a review
   rubric.
7. Iterate by changing one variable at a time when precision matters.

### gpt_image_2_text_guided_editing_methodology

For text-guided image editing, the most reusable prompt pattern is:

`Edit [target area or element] to [specific change] while preserving [identity,
composition, camera angle, lighting, materials, text/logos, and other anchors].
Do not change [protected details].`

Operational rules:

1. State what changes and what must remain identical. The preservation clause is
   as important as the desired edit.
2. Use masks for localized edits when the interface/API supports them, but
   treat masks as guidance rather than a guarantee of pixel-perfect boundaries.
3. Put the most identity-critical reference image first when the model or API
   gives higher fidelity to earlier images.
4. Use small, sequential edits for faces, logos, product labels, and branded
   assets. After each edit, verify identity, proportions, text, and lighting.
5. For retouching, ask for natural texture preservation and avoid
   over-smoothing, face reshaping, fake skin, or unrelated beautification.
6. For commercial assets, request realistic shadows, grounded contact points,
   consistent reflections, and no new text unless text is explicitly specified.

### gpt_image_2_image_reference_workflows

Image-reference editing and image-to-image generation should use labeled
references and clear role separation:

1. Label references by role: `Image 1 = subject identity`, `Image 2 = style
   reference`, `Image 3 = background/location`, `Image 4 = product/detail`.
2. Define which reference controls identity, which controls style, and which
   controls environment. Do not let a style reference override identity.
3. For character or product consistency, first create a reference sheet before
   generating final scenes. Use front/side/back, close-up, material details,
   color palette, and invariant props.
4. For campaign workflows, approve one hero image, then derive crops,
   background variants, social sizes, thumbnails, and video reference frames
   from the same visual direction.
5. For storyboards, generate beats as individual panels or contact sheets with
   fixed character, wardrobe, location, and camera-language anchors.

### prompt_review_rubric_for_creative_outputs

Every generated or edited image should be reviewed against a compact rubric:

- Intent fit: Does the image solve the requested asset purpose?
- Preservation: Are identity, logos, labels, product geometry, and important
  reference details intact?
- Physical plausibility: Are shadows, reflections, perspective, hands, fabric,
  hair, and contact points believable?
- Text quality: Is all visible text exact, legible, correctly placed, and free
  of duplicate or invented words?
- Edit boundary: Did the model change only the target area?
- Commercial readiness: Is the output clean enough for e-commerce, social,
  print, video, or client review?
- Safety/IP: Does it avoid unsafe, deceptive, privacy-violating, trademark-
  risky, or copied-artist/copyrighted-character outputs?

### photoshop_mask_and_adjustment_layer_workflow

Targeted retouching should prefer masks and adjustment layers before permanent
pixel edits:

1. Use adjustment layers for color and tonal changes so the original pixels
   remain recoverable.
2. Use layer masks to confine corrections. White reveals, black conceals, and
   gray creates partial transparency for gradual transitions.
3. Build masks from selections, Color Range, object selection, or hand painting,
   then refine with soft brushes and density/feather controls.
4. Use Curves and Levels for local exposure/contrast control, Hue/Saturation or
   Selective Color for localized color repair, and Gradient Map or Color Lookup
   only after local corrections are stable.
5. Keep global grading above retouching layers so cleanup remains separable
   from final look development.

### photoshop_object_removal_decision_tree

Object removal should use the smallest tool that can solve the defect:

1. Spot Healing or Healing Brush for small dust, blemishes, and texture defects.
2. Clone Stamp when the replacement texture must be manually sourced.
3. Remove tool for larger distractions, with `Sample All Layers` enabled when
   working non-destructively and multiple strokes for complex regions.
4. Content-Aware Fill when selection control and surrounding-pixel sampling are
   more important than generative invention.
5. Generative Fill when the missing region requires semantic reconstruction,
   new objects, or plausible background creation.
6. Manual reconciliation after AI fill: inspect repeating patterns, broken
   perspective, edge halos, contact shadows, texture mismatch, and invented
   details.

### photoshop_professional_retouching_order

Professional portrait and beauty retouching tutorials converge on a repeatable
layer order:

1. Raw/base correction outside Photoshop when needed: exposure, white balance,
   lens correction, and rough crop.
2. Pixel cleanup on separate layers: blemishes, dust, flyaway hair, clothing
   lint, background distractions.
3. Structure-safe corrections: cautious Liquify as a smart-object or merged
   copy when proportions truly need adjustment.
4. Frequency separation only for texture/tone separation problems, not as a
   global smoothing shortcut.
5. Curve-based dodge and burn for skin transitions, dimension, and natural
   shape.
6. Local color correction for redness, blotchiness, makeup, lips, hair,
   wardrobe, and product-specific colors.
7. Global look: contrast, color grade, grain, sharpening, vignette, and export.

### photoshop_product_retouching_methodology

Product retouching shares the same non-destructive principles but has different
failure modes:

1. Preserve product geometry, label placement, typography, color, material, and
   edge silhouette.
2. Remove dust, scratches, fingerprints, packaging dents, background seams, and
   reflection clutter before adding polish.
3. Use frequency separation for product surfaces when texture and tone need
   independent repair, such as noisy backgrounds, fabric, leather, cosmetics,
   packaging, or reflective surfaces.
4. Rebuild shadows and reflections cautiously. Contact shadows must anchor the
   product and reflections must match perspective and material.
5. Treat labels, logos, regulatory text, serial numbers, and brand colors as
   protected details requiring manual verification after any AI edit.

### openai_image_eval_harness_methodology

OpenAI Cookbook image-eval guidance should be part of the creative skill base
because it turns subjective image review into repeatable production QA:

1. Define test cases with prompt, task type, criteria, and optional input images
   or masks.
2. Separate runners from graders so the same rubric can compare different
   models, prompts, quality settings, or edit strategies.
3. Score hard constraints first: exact text, count, attributes, locality,
   preservation, and region-specific edits.
4. Add graded perceptual criteria only after hard failures are controlled:
   realism, sharpness, aesthetic fit, brand fit, and character/style
   consistency.
5. Tag failure modes so prompt or workflow changes are targeted rather than
   random.
6. Use human labels and pairwise preference checks for subjective creative
   direction, but keep non-negotiable constraints as gates.

### openai_high_input_fidelity_editing_methodology

OpenAI high input fidelity examples provide a strong pattern for preserving
faces, logos, and distinctive details during edits:

1. Use high input fidelity for identity-critical image edits, especially faces,
   logos, product marks, and personalized avatars.
2. Keep edit prompts narrow for isolated changes: color swaps, element removal,
   or single-object insertion.
3. Preserve unrelated areas explicitly in the prompt and verify that the output
   did not drift in composition, lighting, identity, or protected text.
4. For multi-image edits, define the role of each input image and check whether
   the model gives higher fidelity to earlier images in the chosen API/model.
5. Prefer high quality when visual precision matters; lower quality is only a
   latency/cost tradeoff for drafts.

### openai_cookbook_production_prompt_patterns

OpenAI Cookbook image-generation examples show several reusable production
patterns:

1. Generate structured visuals such as infographics, diagrams, UI concepts,
   logos, comic panels, or storybook scenes with explicit layout and text
   constraints.
2. Translate or revise text-bearing images with instructions to preserve all
   non-text aspects.
3. Use style transfer by providing a style reference and specifying what should
   change versus remain controlled.
4. For story continuation, reuse the approved previous image and explicitly
   constrain the model not to redesign the character.
5. For localized editing, use prompts that name the target object or region and
   preserve camera angle, lighting, shadows, surrounding objects, and material
   texture.
6. Treat quality and input-fidelity parameters as workflow controls, not just
   output decoration.

### photoshop_compositing_believability_methodology

Composite images should be judged by physical coherence before style:

1. Match perspective, camera height, lens feel, scale, horizon line, and object
   contact points before color grading.
2. Diagnose the background light source: direction, height, hardness, color
   temperature, intensity, and shadow length.
3. Rebuild or transfer shadows separately from highlights. Multiply-style
   shadow layers and Screen/Lighten-style highlight layers can preserve natural
   contrast when adjusted with masks and opacity.
4. Match ambient color and contrast so inserted subjects do not look cut out.
   Use Curves, Levels, Color Balance, Hue/Saturation, Match Color, and clipped
   adjustment layers.
5. Add contact shadows first, then cast shadows, then reflected light. Contact
   shadows usually matter more than dramatic long shadows for grounding.
6. Use blur, grain/noise, edge cleanup, chromatic aberration, and depth of
   field only after geometry and light are believable.
7. Reject source combinations that cannot plausibly match. Some composites fail
   because the source perspective or lighting is incompatible, not because the
   retoucher lacks a tool.

### photoshop_old_photo_restoration_methodology

Old-photo restoration should prioritize preservation before enhancement:

1. Start from the highest-quality scan possible. Avoid low-bit-depth,
   over-compressed, auto-corrected, clipped, or document-scanner JPEG sources
   when better scanning is possible.
2. Lock or preserve the original background layer and perform repairs on
   separate layers.
3. Use Photo Restoration Neural Filter or similar AI restoration only as an
   initial pass, then adjust strength for face enhancement and scratch
   reduction.
4. Repair remaining damage with Spot Healing, Healing Brush, Clone Stamp, Patch
   Tool, Dust & Scratches, and small brush strokes. Avoid broad strokes that
   smear texture or blend across high-contrast edges.
5. For faded color photos, use Levels, Curves, Hue/Saturation, Color Balance,
   and selective masks to restore tonal separation and color plausibility.
6. For black-and-white or sepia photos, use Colorize as a draft and then
   manually refine skin, hair, clothing, background, and object colors with
   color blend layers or masks.
7. Preserve historical plausibility. Colorization should use period and context
   references rather than arbitrary modern colors.

### photoshop_ecommerce_asset_workflow

E-commerce image work needs repeatability more than dramatic style:

1. Normalize product cutouts, canvas size, background color, margins, and
   perspective across a catalog.
2. Preserve SKU-specific details: label text, packaging folds, colorways,
   materials, stitching, buttons, logos, serial information, and product shape.
3. Use clean white or transparent backgrounds only after keeping realistic
   grounding shadows where the marketplace or brand allows.
4. Separate shadow types: natural shadow, drop shadow, reflection shadow, and
   cast shadow. Choose one intentionally instead of mixing them accidentally.
5. Batch what is safe: alignment, export format, background cleanup, and global
   color targets. Avoid batching identity-critical label/logo reconstruction
   without review.
6. Maintain a final inspection checklist for edge halos, dust, jagged masks,
   warped labels, missing reflections, inconsistent shadow direction, and color
   mismatch across variants.

### segmentation_assisted_image_editing_methodology

Segmentation-assisted editing is useful when prompt-only edits are too broad:

1. Use segmentation or selection tools to define the target region before
   inpainting or generative editing.
2. Export masks with correct size, format, and alpha channel when the image API
   or editing tool requires it.
3. Treat masks as guidance rather than absolute protection unless the tool
   explicitly guarantees hard-boundary preservation.
4. Use the prompt to describe the full desired final image, not only the masked
   hole, so the model can match context.
5. For multi-object scenes, segment the exact object instance to avoid editing
   the wrong repeated object.
6. After generation, verify locality: changed target area, unchanged protected
   area, no composition drift, no invented text/logos, and no identity drift.

### source_plate_capture_for_retouching

For future workflow design, the creative skill system should remember that
better source capture reduces retouching burden:

1. For product work, capture clean plates, reflection-control plates, shadow
   plates, and alternate exposure plates when possible.
2. Use flags, cards, diffusers, and polarizing control during the shoot instead
   of assuming every reflection can be fixed later.
3. For composites, choose source images with compatible light direction,
   camera height, focal length, resolution, and perspective.
4. For restoration, request better scans before attempting aggressive repair.
5. For AI image editing, collect identity, product, style, and background
   references as separate labeled inputs instead of relying on one ambiguous
   image.

### civil_construction_site_image_editing_methodology

Engineering construction site retouching should preserve construction logic,
not merely make a scene prettier:

1. Identify the construction stage before editing: pre-excavation layout,
   pavement breaking, trench excavation, bedding, pipe laying, jointing,
   inspection chamber/vault work, initial backfill, compaction, pavement
   reinstatement, or final cleanup.
2. Preserve scale cues: trench width, pipe diameter, bedding thickness,
   excavator bucket size, worker height, safety barriers, traffic cones,
   manhole rings, sheet pile module width, and vehicle track width.
3. Preserve engineering alignment: pipe slope, trench centerline, conduit
   spacing, road lane direction, curb line, utility corridor, and foundation pit
   geometry.
4. Keep soil and surface layers believable: asphalt surface, aggregate base,
   subgrade, bedding sand/gravel, excavated spoil piles, muddy water, trench
   walls, and compacted lifts should not blur into one generic dirt texture.
5. Do not invent unsafe or misleading conditions in documentation-style images.
   If an edit is conceptual or illustrative, label it as such outside the image
   workflow.

### road_construction_and_pavement_retouching_methodology

Road construction photo edits should be perspective-aware:

1. Use vanishing-point or perspective-warp workflows when extending road
   surfaces, adding asphalt patches, placing lane markings, aligning barriers,
   or moving construction equipment.
2. Match asphalt texture scale, aggregate grain, cracks, tar seams, tire marks,
   dust, edge feathering, and rolling/compaction marks.
3. Road markings must follow pavement perspective, width, dashed-line spacing,
   paint wear, and occlusion by dust, water, workers, or machinery.
4. When cleaning pavement, remove distractions on separate layers but preserve
   realistic surface variation; over-cleaned construction pavement looks fake.
5. For wet road or muddy site edits, reflections and puddles need local
   perspective, darker contact edges, sediment coloration, and plausible
   drainage direction.

### trench_and_pipe_laying_visual_semantics

Open-cut pipe installation images should respect the normal trench sequence and
visual zones:

1. Trench bottom/foundation: stable base, sometimes sub-excavation or
   replacement material in poor soil.
2. Bedding layer: granular sand, pea gravel, crushed stone, or specified
   bedding supporting the pipe uniformly.
3. Pipe zone: water pipe, sewer pipe, storm pipe, corrugated HDPE pipe,
   corrugated metal pipe, or conduit placed with correct alignment and joint
   orientation.
4. Haunching and initial backfill: material placed around the lower sides and
   above the pipe crown, compacted in controlled lifts.
5. Utility marker layer: warning tape/tracer layer placed above the service,
   not directly on the pipe, where specified.
6. Final backfill and surface restoration: compacted material up to pavement
   structure, sidewalk, road base, landscaping, or temporary road plate.
7. Inspection elements: manholes, catch basins, valve boxes, vaults,
   handholes, couplers, gaskets, pipe bells/spigots, lifting slings, grade
   lasers, and survey marks.

### corrugated_pipe_installation_image_rules

Corrugated pipe imagery has distinctive features that should be preserved:

1. Corrugation rhythm should follow pipe curvature and perspective. AI edits
   often fail by making ridges irregular, melted, or inconsistent.
2. Pipe bedding must support the pipe barrel uniformly; hard rocks or debris
   directly under the pipe are visual red flags unless showing a bad example.
3. Bell-and-spigot or coupler joints should align with pipe axis and not float,
   kink, or reverse direction.
4. Backfill should appear symmetrical around flexible pipes where the scene
   implies proper installation; uneven heavy fill on one side can imply
   deformation risk.
5. Large corrugated metal or HDPE pipes require lifting equipment, slings, or
   excavator placement cues. A very large pipe hand-placed by workers alone may
   look implausible.

### electrical_duct_bank_and_power_conduit_visual_rules

Underground electrical duct bank images require different semantics from water
or drainage pipe images:

1. Multiple conduits are commonly organized in rows or tiers, held by spacers
   to maintain uniform horizontal and vertical separation.
2. Concrete encasement or controlled backfill may surround the conduit bank;
   edits should not leave conduits randomly floating in soil if the task asks
   for a duct bank.
3. Vault, manhole, handhole, and sweep interfaces should align with conduit
   bundles and bend radii.
4. Warning tape, tracer wire, pull lines, labels, and color-coded conduit are
   important utility-identification cues.
5. Duct bank edits should preserve straightness and pullability; sharp kinks,
   inconsistent spacing, crushed conduits, or impossible bends are visual
   failures.

### sheet_pile_cross_strut_shoring_visual_rules

Larssen/sheet pile trench or foundation-pit support imagery should show a
coherent support system:

1. Sheet piles form a continuous vertical interlocked wall; the repeated
   corrugated steel profile should be consistent in spacing, height, and
   perspective.
2. Walers run along the sheet pile wall and distribute load from struts to the
   wall.
3. Horizontal struts or pipe/I-beam cross braces span between opposing walers.
   Their ends should visibly bear on brackets, jacking plates, or waler
   connections instead of floating.
4. Braced excavation is staged: sheet piles are installed first, soil is
   excavated to a support level, walers/struts are installed and possibly
   preloaded, then excavation continues.
5. Cross braces consume workspace. If the edit adds pipe installation, worker
   access, or equipment inside a braced trench, check whether the struts block
   the implied operation.
6. For deeper excavations, multiple support levels may be present; shallow
   trenches may use trench boxes, hydraulic shoring, sloping/benching, or
   sheet piles depending on context.

### foundation_pit_widening_and_trench_expansion_rules

When editing a trench or foundation pit to appear widened, the whole scene must
change consistently:

1. Pit edge, pavement cut line, curb line, and soil face move together.
2. Existing utilities, pipes, steel supports, ladders, barriers, cones, spoil
   piles, and excavator reach must be repositioned or occluded consistently.
3. Sheet pile or shoring width changes require longer or repositioned struts,
   changed waler spans, and adjusted access space.
4. Widening a trench changes shadow geometry and exposed soil area. It cannot
   be a simple content-aware expansion of the hole.
5. For documentation edits, do not alter apparent excavation dimensions without
   retaining the original source and marking the result as illustrative.

### construction_site_output_review_rubric

Construction-site generated or edited images should be reviewed with a domain
rubric:

- Geometry: trench edges, road perspective, pipe axis, sheet pile spacing, and
  support lines are coherent.
- Scale: workers, equipment, pipe diameter, trench width, barriers, and
  shoring members match plausible proportions.
- Construction sequence: the scene matches a real work stage and does not mix
  incompatible stages without explanation.
- Utility semantics: water/drainage/electrical/telecom elements are visually
  distinct and not randomly combined.
- Safety: PPE, access/egress, traffic control, unsupported trench walls,
  suspended loads, and equipment-at-edge hazards are not made misleading.
- Material fidelity: asphalt, soil, bedding, corrugated pipe, steel sheet pile,
  conduit, concrete, mud, and water have distinct textures and contact shadows.
- Documentation integrity: original facts are not changed in a way that could
  misrepresent actual site conditions unless the image is explicitly
  conceptual.

### construction_site_reference_selection_rules

When using reference images for GPT Image or Photoshop edits:

1. Use a same-stage reference first. A pipe-laying reference is better than a
   finished-road reference when editing a trench scene.
2. Match camera height and angle. Trench interiors, top-down utility photos,
   street-level roadworks, and drone progress photos have different geometry.
3. Match ground and climate. Dry asphalt, wet clay, sandy bedding, compacted
   aggregate, and flooded trenches require different textures and reflections.
4. Match jurisdictional visual cues where relevant: traffic-control style,
   PPE colors, warning tape colors, road markings, curb types, and signage.
5. Prefer real project photos, manuals, and engineering photo albums over
   stock images when technical plausibility matters.
6. Use stock photos only for visual texture and composition ideas, not for
   engineering correctness unless supported by a technical source.

### construction_site_negative_prompt_and_failure_modes

Construction prompts and edits should explicitly avoid common model failures:

- Random unsupported trench walls where shoring should exist.
- Pipes floating above bedding or cutting through trench walls.
- Corrugated pipe ridges melting, changing spacing, or wrapping in impossible
  directions.
- Sheet piles with inconsistent interlocks, wavy wall lines, or disconnected
  struts.
- Cross braces that do not bear on walers or pass through pipes/workers.
- Electrical duct banks with chaotic conduit spacing, impossible bend radii, or
  missing spacers.
- Warning tape placed directly on a pipe or twisted into unreadable strips.
- Workers without PPE in scenes that are meant to show compliant work.
- Excavators sitting too close to unsupported trench edges in a misleading
  documentation-style image.
- Over-cleaned construction scenes with no dust, tire marks, spoil piles,
  staging materials, or realistic clutter.

### construction_site_photoshop_layer_stack

A practical Photoshop layer stack for construction-site edits:

1. `source_locked`: untouched original image.
2. `survey_guides`: perspective lines, trench centerline, pipe axis, horizon,
   scale marks, and protected zones.
3. `cleanup`: separate layer for debris, sensor spots, unwanted signs, or
   temporary clutter that is not engineering evidence.
4. `geometry_change`: masked edits for trench widening, surface extension,
   road patching, pipe placement, or shoring additions.
5. `engineering_objects`: pipes, conduits, sheet piles, walers, struts,
   warning tape, barriers, and equipment.
6. `contact_shadows`: separate multiply layers for pipes, braces, equipment,
   workers, trench walls, and bedding contact.
7. `material_match`: clipped Curves/Levels/Hue/Saturation for soil, asphalt,
   steel, PVC/HDPE, concrete, and mud color matching.
8. `site_atmosphere`: dust, water, haze, tire marks, small aggregate, and
   surface wear.
9. `safety_review_marks`: temporary check layer for PPE, trench protection,
   access, edge clearance, and documentation warnings.
10. `final_grade`: restrained contrast, clarity, color, sharpening, and export.

### construction_site_before_after_integrity_workflow

### construction_photo_evidence_handling_rule

Construction-site image editing must classify the image role before any edit:

1. Evidence photo: original field record for inspection, progress, safety,
   claim, payment, defect, or as-built documentation. Preserve unchanged.
2. Evidence markup: original photo plus annotations, arrows, measurements, or
   callouts. Markups must be reversible and clearly distinguish annotation from
   pixel alteration.
3. Presentation cleanup: cleaned image for reports or client communication.
   Keep original linked and do not remove defects/hazards if the image could be
   interpreted as evidence.
4. Concept visualization: proposed or hypothetical future state. Label as
   concept, simulation, draft, or illustrative outside the image workflow.
5. Training/reference image: used to teach prompt or visual style. Track source
   license and do not imply it documents the user's project.

For construction photos that may become evidence:

2. Store edited derivatives with a new filename and clear suffix such as
   `concept`, `markup`, `presentation-cleanup`, or `ai-visualization`.
3. Keep prompt, mask, reference-image list, and edit notes with AI derivatives.
4. Do not overwrite originals. Do not strip metadata from evidence originals.
5. For before/after comparisons, use matched viewpoints when possible and

### construction_ai_prompt_quality_filter

Public construction-site AI prompts should be ranked by technical specificity:

- High value: mentions work stage, trench/pipe/shoring object, material zone,
  camera viewpoint, scale cues, safety context, and forbidden failure modes.
- Medium value: gives realistic site atmosphere, equipment, workers, lighting,
  and composition but lacks engineering layer logic.
- Low value: generic cinematic construction site prompt with cranes,
  bulldozers, hard hats, sunrise, dust, and no task-specific construction
  semantics.

The creative skill system should convert useful low/medium examples into
structured prompts instead of storing them verbatim.

### drone_orthomosaic_cad_overlay_rules

Drone and CAD/BIM overlay workflows need a separate rule set from ordinary
Photoshop compositing:

1. Use orthomosaic or georeferenced imagery for map-like overlays, progress
   comparisons, area takeoffs, stockpile analysis, and design-vs-built review.
2. Use oblique drone photos for visual communication, inspection context, and
   presentation, not precise plan overlay unless camera geometry is solved.
3. Align CAD/DXF/PDF overlays using known reference points, coordinate systems,
   ground control points, or clearly identifiable site features.
4. Preserve source coordinate/projection metadata when the result will be used
   for measurement or engineering review.
5. When exporting a Photoshop visualization from drone imagery, label whether
   it is survey-grade, approximate overlay, or presentation-only.
6. Do not manually warp a design drawing over a site photo and present it as an
   accurate as-built comparison.

### construction_photo_annotation_and_markup_rules

Engineering photo markups should communicate clearly without altering evidence:

1. Use arrows for target identification, boxes/circles for areas, leader lines
   for callouts, and dimension lines for known measurements.
2. Keep annotation color and line weight readable but not confused with actual
   painted site markings, utility marks, warning tape, or design linework.
3. Put uncertain interpretation in a note, not as a factual label.
4. Use separate markup layers or exported derivatives so the original remains
   unchanged.
5. For dimensions, state whether the value is measured on site, taken from a
   drawing, estimated from a scale reference, or illustrative.
6. Include scale references in future site photos when dimensional markups will
   be needed: measuring tape, staff, ruler, known pipe diameter, survey rod, or
   marked control point.

### civil_site_photo_measurement_limits

The creative skill system should not infer precise construction dimensions
from arbitrary photos unless a valid measurement basis exists:

1. A known reference object must be in the same plane/depth as the measured
   target, or the result is only approximate.
2. Perspective correction can make lines look straight but does not restore
   missing 3D information.
3. Lens distortion and parallax can invalidate simple pixel-to-real-world
   ratios.
4. Drone orthomosaics, calibrated photogrammetry, or survey data are preferred
   for distance/area measurement.
5. Any AI-generated measurement mark should be treated as visual annotation,
   not evidence.

### engineering_visualization_output_classes

Engineering image outputs should be classified by intended use:

- `presentation`: cleaned, clear, visually readable, not a record of exact
  conditions.
- `concept`: proposed future work or hypothetical site condition.
- `markup`: original or near-original photo with arrows, dimensions, and notes.
- `overlay`: CAD/BIM/design overlay on orthomosaic or site photo, with accuracy
  level stated.
- `progress-comparison`: before/after or time-series visual, ideally from
  matched viewpoints or georeferenced drone data.
- `evidence`: unmodified original or minimally processed documentation image.

## Do Not Persist

### low_signal_prompt_dumps

Do not persist raw prompt dumps, viral examples, ranking claims, or one-off
social posts as durable memory unless they are converted into a source-backed
principle, workflow pattern, or review criterion.

### unverifiable_model_claims

Do not persist third-party claims about GPT Image 2 pricing, hidden internal
architecture, secret capabilities, or exact deployment behavior unless confirmed
by official OpenAI documentation or direct controlled testing.

### unsafe_or_rights_violating_workflows

Do not preserve instructions for bypassing safety systems, generating deceptive
identity edits, copying living artists' protected styles on demand, removing
watermarks, impersonating real people, or misusing copyrighted/trademarked
assets.

### generic_construction_prompt_pages

Do not persist generic construction-site prompt pages as authoritative durable
knowledge. They can inspire vocabulary, but they usually lack utility trench,
pipe bedding, duct bank, sheet pile, cross-strut, backfill, compaction, and
construction documentation integrity rules.

## Risk Notes

### memory_write_unavailable

The live Hermes Memory Fabric write-proposal MCP tools were unavailable in this
session, so this document is only a local governed staging artifact. It does not
create durable memory, operation-ledger entries, graph nodes, policy changes,
or real write proposals.

### source_recency_and_model_drift

Image-generation model behavior changes quickly. Entries about GPT Image 2,
ChatGPT Images 2.0, editing fidelity, supported parameters, and availability
should be rechecked against official OpenAI documentation before production
implementation.

### third_party_prompt_repository_quality

GitHub prompt repositories vary widely in quality and may contain copied,
unsafe, SEO-oriented, or model-specific prompts. They should be mined for
patterns, not ingested verbatim.

### tutorial_quality_filter

Tutorials should be ranked higher when they provide ordered workflow,
non-destructive layer strategy, before/after reasoning, failure modes, and
repeatable review criteria. Tutorials should be ranked lower when they promise
one-click beauty, hide tradeoffs, overuse smoothing, lack source files, or
encourage unverified AI edits on identity-critical or brand-critical details.

### compositing_source_mismatch_risk

The creative skill system should not imply that any bad composite can be fixed
with enough Photoshop work. Some source images have incompatible perspective,
lighting, resolution, motion blur, or focal length. In those cases, the correct
recommendation may be to reshoot, find a better source, generate a better
reference, or change the composition.

### restoration_authenticity_risk

Old-photo restoration and colorization can easily become reinterpretation.
Restoration workflows should distinguish repair, enhancement, and creative
colorization, especially for family, archival, journalistic, legal, or
historical materials.

### construction_documentation_integrity_risk

Construction-site edits can affect engineering, safety, payment, claims, or
inspection interpretation. The creative skill system must distinguish
illustrative/conceptual edits from factual construction documentation. It
should avoid altering apparent dimensions, shoring adequacy, PPE compliance,
buried utility position, pipe installation quality, or excavation safety in a
way that could misrepresent real site conditions.

### civil_engineering_authority_risk

Photoshop and GPT Image workflows can help visualize construction scenarios but
must not replace civil, geotechnical, structural, utility, electrical, or safety
engineering judgment. Shoring, trench depth, pipe bedding, duct bank spacing,
warning tape depth, and excavation safety must be checked against project
drawings, local codes, manufacturer manuals, and licensed professional review
where required.
